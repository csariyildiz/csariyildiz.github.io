## Jean Piaget

Jean Piaget, a Swiss psychologist, is known for his work on cognitive development in children. He proposed four stages of cognitive development: sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational. Piaget believed that play was an essential part of children’s cognitive development and that children engage in different types of play that reflect their level of cognitive development. 

Piaget also studied the development of games in children and how they relate to cognitive development. In his theory of play, games are the last manifestation of children’s ludic activity.

Piaget’s work on cognitive development and play has been influential in the field of psychology and continues to be studied.

## How accurate is IQ?

IQ tests provide an estimate of the construct defined as “intelligence”, but neither are IQ scores “perfect” measures, nor do IQ scores capture the full range of abilities that refer to a person’s intellectual abilities. However, studies have shown that people with higher IQs tend to earn higher incomes, on average, than those with lower IQs 2. High IQs are comparably reliable in predicting academic success, job performance, career potential and creativity 2. In fact, according to psychologist John Antonakis, IQ is the single most important predictor of work success 2. However, personality traits such as being outgoing and friendly, self-confidence, being open to new experiences, organizational abilities and strong communication skills are also important factors that can help you get ahead in life.

## Freudian Concepts

Sigmund Freud, the father of psychoanalysis, introduced several influential concepts that have shaped the field of psychology. Here are some of the most important ones:

* Unconscious mind: Freud believed that the mind is a reservoir of thoughts, memories, and emotions that lie outside the awareness of the conscious mind.
* Personality: Freud proposed that personality was made up of three key elements: the id, the ego, and the superego.
* Psychosexual stages of development: Freud suggested that children go through a series of psychosexual stages during their development, each characterized by a different focus on erogenous zones.
* Oedipus complex: This concept refers to a child’s unconscious sexual desire for their opposite-sex parent and rivalry with their same-sex parent.
* Defense mechanisms: Freud believed that people use defense mechanisms to protect themselves from unpleasant emotions such as anxiety and guilt.

Some of Freud's major books include:

* Totem and Taboo

## Myers-Briggs MBTI

The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) is an assessment of personality based on questions about a person’s preferences in four domains: focusing outward or inward; attending to sensory information or adding interpretation; deciding by logic or by situation; and making judgments or remaining open to information. The MBTI was initially developed in the 1940s by Katharine Cook Briggs and her daughter, Isabell Briggs Myers, loosely based on a personality typology created by psychoanalyst Carl Jung.

When responses are scored, the assessment yields a psychological “type” summarized in four letters, one for each preference: Extraversion (E) or Introversion (I); Sensing (S) or Intuiting (N); Thinking (T) or Feeling (F); and Judging (J) or Perceiving (P). The results combined into one of 16 possible type descriptions, such as ENTJ or ISFP.

While the MBTI is used by many organizations to select new personnel and has been taken millions of times, personality psychologists and other scientists report that it has relatively little scientific validity. Psychologists who investigate personality typically rely on scientifically developed assessments of traits clustered into five (the Big Five) or six (HEXACO) domains.

## Classifying Fantasies

William Hartman and Marilyn Fithian in the 1980s proposed a taxonomy of sexual fantasies based on their research. Their classification included three categories:

* Exploratory Fantasies: These involve novelty, experimentation, and curiosity. They often include scenarios where people explore new sexual experiences or partners.
* Exhibitionistic Fantasies: These fantasies are about being the center of attention, often in a sexual context. This may include scenarios like public sex or performing for an audience.
* Affectionate Fantasies: These involve emotional connection and intimacy. These fantasies often focus on deep emotional bonds and relationships
