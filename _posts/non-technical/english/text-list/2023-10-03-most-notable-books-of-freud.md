Sigmund Freud (1856-1939) was an Austrian neurologist and the founder of psychoanalysis, a clinical method for evaluating and treating pathologies seen as originating from conflicts in the psyche, 
through dialogue between patient and psychoanalyst, and the distinctive theory of mind and human agency derived from it.

Freud's work has had a profound impact on our understanding of the human mind, and his ideas continue to be debated and discussed today. 
He is best known for his theories about the unconscious mind, sexuality, and personality development.

Freud's theories were controversial in his own time, and they remain so today. 
Freud's work has been criticized for being unscientific and for being based on his own personal experiences. 
However, there is no doubt that he was one of the most influential thinkers of the 20th century. 
His work has changed the way we think about ourselves and the way we treat mental illness.

The most notable books of Sigmund Freud are:

* The Interpretation of Dreams (1900)
* Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality (1905)
* Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious (1905)
* Psychopathology of Everyday Life (1901)
* Totem and Taboo (1913)
* The Ego and the Id (1923)
* Civilization and Its Discontents (1930)

* The Interpretation of Dreams is Freud's most famous book, and it is considered to be one of the most important works of psychology ever written. 
In this book, Freud argues that dreams are not just random nonsense, but rather that they have a hidden meaning that can be interpreted to reveal our unconscious thoughts and desires.

* Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality is another of Freud's most important works. In this book, Freud argues that sexuality is a central part of human development, 
and that it begins in early childhood. He also introduces the concept of the Oedipus complex, which is a theory about how children develop sexual and aggressive feelings towards their parents.

* Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious is a book in which Freud explores the relationship between jokes and the unconscious mind. 
He argues that jokes are a way of expressing unconscious thoughts and desires that would otherwise be repressed.

* Psychopathology of Everyday Life is a book in which Freud examines the everyday mistakes and slips that people make, such as forgetting names or losing keys. 
He argues that these mistakes are not just accidents, but rather that they have a hidden meaning that can be interpreted to reveal our unconscious thoughts and desires.

* Totem and Taboo is a book in which Freud explores the relationship between totemism and taboo in primitive societies. 
He argues that these practices are a way of dealing with the unconscious guilt and anxiety that arises from the Oedipus complex.

* The Ego and the Id is a book in which Freud introduces his structural model of the psyche, which consists of the id, the ego, and the superego. 
He argues that these three parts of the psyche are in constant conflict with each other, and that this conflict is the source of much of our psychological distress.

* Civilization and Its Discontents is a book in which Freud explores the relationship between civilization and individual happiness. 
He argues that civilization requires us to repress our natural instincts, which leads to a certain amount of unhappiness.

## The Most Important Technical Works

Consider these if you really want to understand Freud and are willing to put in the effort.

* The Neuro-Psychoses of Defense (SE III)
* The Interpretation of Dreams, II-III and VI-VII (SE IV-V)
* Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality (SE VII)
* Papers on Metapsychology (SE XIV)
* Beyond the Pleasure Principle (SE XVIII)
* The Ego and the Id (SE XIX)
* Symptoms, Inhibitions, and Anxiety (SE XX)

## The popular works:

Consider these if you're interested in more literary work which you're more likely to encounter referenced in your general reading.

* Totem and Taboo (SE XIII)
* The Future of an Illusion (SE XXI)
* Civilization and Its Discontents (SE XXI)
* Moses and Monotheism (SE XXIII)

## The Major Case Studies

* Studies on Hysteria [incl. "Anna O"] (SE II, with Breuer)
* Fragment of an Analysis of a Case of Hysteria ["Dora"] (SE VII)
* Analysis of a Phobia in a Five-Year-Old Boy ["Little Hans"] (SE X)
* Notes Upon a Case of Obsessional Neurosis ["Rat Man"] (SE X)
* Psycho-Analytic Notes on an Autobiographical Account of a Case of Paranoia ["Schreber"] (SE XII)
* From the History of an Infantile Neurosis ["Wolf Man"] (SE XVII)
